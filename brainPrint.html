3D Printing My Own Brain

In 2014 there came a time that I was in need of a full head MRI scan in order to see what kind of shape my brain was in. I saw this as an opportunity to make something interesting and extremely unique. I decided that I would attempt to turn the data into a 3D printable model and print it out at a 1:1 scale. At the time, 3D printing was still relatively new, especially medical 3D printing, therefor there wasn't a concrete method at the time to turn MRI scan data into a usable 3D model however I wouldn't let this deter me. At the time of the scan I requested for a copy of the data to be made for me to be picked up. It came in the form of a CD disk with a commercial MRI viewer installed to present the data to the user. I extracted the data that it was displaying from the program and stored it in a file for my use. There was information from 4 different types of scans that they performed at the same time. One was a relatively detailed albeit small resolution scan, while the other were each high definition and high resolution in one direction: front to back, side to side, and top to bottom. I ended up choosing the scan for printing through trial and error to see which one would look the best when scaled up to 1:1 which was somewhat of an arduous process. In order to compile the data into a 3D model I used a program called OsiriX MD to make the rough model in conjunction with Simpleware's ScanIP to clean it up and fix all of the errors in the model. Once the model was solidified I printed it at 1:1 ratio with 10% infill which took 48 hours to print.
